Ferris Acres

The benefits of an online ordering system

Psychedelicatessen

Designing an inventory management system

Ferris Acres Creamery has become very successful in recent years. In an effort to combat their growing pains, my team and I developed an online ordering system for ice cream cakes and pints in order to address their demand and achieve maximum efficiency.

MY ROLE

Project Manager, Financial Manager, & UI/UX developer


PROJECT DURATION

Fall 2016


DEMO

THE PROBLEM

During operations, one staff member is dedicated to taking orders by phone. While this system works for prepacked pints and quarts, it has not scaled well for cake orders. Each order takes at least 15 minutes as the employee guides the customer through a myriad of options. Cakes themselves take an additional 40 minutes of preparation time.

In a business already struggling to keep up with demand, a night laden with cake orders can slow operations significantly, resulting in a significant loss of efficiency and a potential loss of sales.

Our proposed solution aims to assist Ferris Acres Creamery with their cake and large ice cream orders. The application is designed to automate the process of gathering customer orders. By capturing orders online, it helps decrease the time and resources spent in collecting this information via the phone and in-person. This in turn will allow Ferris Acres Creamery to collect data from more customers more efficiently. Furthermore, by taking the order process online, it provides Ferris Acres Creamery with a consolidated view of the order information along with the means to filter through it.

THE CLIENT

Ferris Acres Creamery is small, family owned ice cream shop located in Southwest Connecticut. The shop itself is unique because it is located within the farm. Visitors are treated to an authentic dairy farm experience, complete with daily cow crossings, a chance to interact with the animals, as well as fresh ice cream created from milk on the farm. Since its opening, the ice cream shop has been a massive success , requiring resources to be pulled from the primary farming business to support it. Numerous local businesses sell the ice cream, and demand continues to grow.

Voted Best Ice Cream in Connecticut by Buzzfeed in 2016

The Ferris family manages the farm as a collective body, with Terry Ferris managing the whole operation. She manages around 100 part-time employees when they are open for the summer season which spans from April through October. Her employees are usually high school students, or college students who are home for the summer, so staff turnover is high. Mrs. Ferris was our primary point of contact for the duration of the project.

REQUIREMENTS

Our initial on site meeting with Mrs. Ferris established the project goals and expectations. It also provided us with a detailed view of the necessary requirements which we used to define the project scope.


FUNCTIONAL

Process

Allow customers to fill out cake order forms on their website
Store this information in a relational backend
Notify the cake builder of the new orders that come in
Allow the employees to update the seasonal flavors
Summarize order information so that informed decisions can be made

People

Employees need to be notified of orders or change in orders
Employees need to know where orders are stored and can be viewed
Employees need to be trained on how the application functions
Customers need to know how to submit a cake order form
Managers need to know how to view and close cake orders

GUI

The user interface of this system had to look modern and fit with the theme of the creamery
The customers should be able to find where and how to place their order with little to no instruction
The cake queue needs to have a simple interface for the floor manager to be able to take and sign off cake orders
The application must be usable on mobile because of their proliferation and the computers available to Ferris Acres Creamery employees while working
The client facing dashboard will display a summary of the orders placed throughout each month in a bar graph format, with each individual order listed below
The orders can be sorted by their completion status: order placed, in progress, and complete, as well as by the date they were placed

NON-FUNCTIONAL

Software

The application is hosted online and serves as a web-page for the employees of the creamery and its potential customers.
The creamery is connected to AT&T as their ISP so that they have network access on their devices to receive notifications of new cake orders
A database connection will be required for the application

Scalability

Staff and future developers have to be able to address any scalability concerns without having to rewrite the application. The Creamery does not anticipate growth on an order of magnitude capable of impacting the application performance in the foreseeable future.

Security

The threats against the system include insecure transactions and reading or modifying sensitive data. Square and the ISP both have multiple security measures to handle these threats. No transaction data will be saved in the application database.
User information must be collected when placing an order. We value user privacy and will put procedures in place to ensure only the bare minimum of information is held, and after three months it is removed in accordance with The Creamery's policy.

Usage

The project will require a small amount of onsite training so that employees are well versed in usage. We will provide materials to facilitate this, and if necessary go on site.

TIMELINE

The 17-week project period was divided into several sprints. We defined the deliverables for each sprint, and held weekly meetings with the client where we discussed our progress. This approach provided the team with feedback and advice on the design and development completed after each sprint. Rapid prototyping was also used with deliverables, and we assigned deliverables to members with the most relevant experience.

As per Murphy's law: anything that can go wrong, will go wrong.

In early October one of our team members resigned from the project. His role was to be our main UI/UX designer as well a front-end developer, and so upon his departure I had to reassign tasks. The remaining members were already heavily involved in the system architecture and development, so I volunteered to assume his role. We had just started the design phase, so it was not difficult to transition into his role. At this time we made the decision to use Bootstrap to simplify the development process.

The development of the prototype was progressing smoothly, until our lead full-stack developer had to take 3 weeks off due to unforseen circumstances. His absence was strongly felt during this time, especially because he was unavailable for the first week. Instead of continuing development at this time, I decided it would be best for us to QA and debug our existing prototype. During the second and third week, our lead developer was available to work remotely and answer questions. Thankfully, my team stayed responsive despite our busy schedules, and we were able to work closely via Facebook Messenger.

IS/IT Design

The final product consists of two interfaces – a customer facing interface and a client facing interface. We decided to build a service-oriented platform on Node JS, which allowed the customer and administrative interface to be built as an online, responsive order form across multiple devices. The customer facing interface has a clean responsive design and is optimized to be used on touch based devices. The client interface places more emphasis on functionality rather than design as it will be used by employees internally. The key aspect of this interface is the database that stores all of the cake and ice cream orders.

WIREFRAMES

COST-BENEFIT ANALYSIS

I conducted a cost-benefit analysis (CBA) to assess the feasibility of the project and to measure the increased efficiency and lowered operational costs. In actuality, we did not receive any payment and this was simply a tool to quantify the projected benefits and to see how practical it would be to implement.

By using a risk-adjusted model, I estimated the internal rate of return (IRR) to be 400% over a five year period. The total cost to be incurred over the same period was estimated to be $22,000, but with an increase in revenue of nearly $200,000. Furthermore, the team also estimated that Ferris Acres would save at least 1000 man hours in their efforts in collecting cake orders.

Costs

The largest non-development related cost incurred was the team and client meetings. With an hourly rate at $26/hour for the client and $20/hour for the team members, the total came to $2232 for a total of $108 hours.
During development in year 0 the total costs came to $11,672, and every subsequent year the cost to maintain the system was estimated to be $2,640 for employee training and maintenance.

Benefits

A benefit was the elimination of a need for a dedicated staff member to answer the phone. This position required 20 hours per week for half the year, at a rate of $15/hour. This comes to a total of $31,200 over the course of 5 years.
The increase in productivity was the largest benefit coming in at $124,800 in increased sales over the course of 5 years. We projected that with the online ordering system, employees would be able to focus on order fulfillment and estimated 20 cones per hour, with each cone averaging $3.
Additional benefits include the system keeping track of the flavors that are being bought, which will save time determining the flavors to purchase more frequently. The analytics will also help the manager optimize shift schedules.
Finally, customers will be more satisfied with the convenience of placing an order online, and picking it up without waiting in line. We predict the increased speed will lead to more satisfied customers and repeat business. We project 1,000 customers valued at $10 each, for a total of $10,000 during the 5 year time frame.

RESULTS & FEEDBACK

Our project resulted in an online ordering form that customers can use to purchase ice cream cakes as well as pints, and a client-facing dashboard displaying all the orders. We made all of our major deadlines and accomplished our outlined requirements, despite losing a teammate. We kept the interface simple and clean, for ease-of-use for both the client and her customers. It was important that her older clients who might not have as much technical experience would be able to use the form. The client-facing interface was also kept simple to keep it intuitive, so it would be easy to train a new employee on the system since Ferris Acres has high staff turnover.

Mrs. Ferris was satisfied with the deliverables, although she noted a couple of outstanding issues, including incorporating all of the data onto our solution. Mrs. Ferris does not think that the application is ready to go live, as there is currently no way to handle payments. She would like to integrate Square with the online ordering system because her pick up window is not equipped to process sales. It is also important that the orders placed online have been pre-paid, to reassure Mrs. Ferris will be compensated for her products.

REFLECTION

An online ordering system would be extremely beneficial to the Creamery, as shown through the CBA. Despite all of the challenges, I was proud of what we had accomplished. Unfortunately we were unable to test a MVP with real customers, but we had created the framework and showed Mrs. Ferris the benefit this would have on her business.

A production level project would require using different tools and different considerations.

At the time of the project, the team members did not have enough experience to approach the problem in a way that could securely handle payments and be production ready.

In retrospect, the team benefitted from the sudden departure of a member during the development phase. This allowed us to regroup and begin testing earlier than we scheduled. By debugging in smaller chunks, I am confident that it led us to fewer issues down the road. We were able to easily fix minor bugs before they became giant, complicated bugs.

The CBA also seems to be inflated, even after changing some numbers to be more realistic at Mrs. Ferris' suggestion. It would be great if we could have compared the actual numbers from a production ready system to see how accurate our projections were. Regardless, it was interesting to calculate the benefits, and I was surprised at how quickly that number added up. I am so thankful to Mrs. Ferris for providing us with this opportunity, making time to give us feedback, and letting us sample her ice cream! I learned so much during this time, and couldn't have asked for a better team and client.
